Sleeping pill tablets are medications designed to help people fall asleep or stay asleep. They come in various types, each working differently to promote sleep. Common Types of Sleeping Pills:

Benzodiazepines (e.g., Temazepam, Diazepam)

Used for short-term sleep issues but can cause dependency if used long-term.

Non-benzodiazepine sedative-hypnotics (e.g., Zolpidem, Zaleplon)

Less addictive and often prescribed for short-term insomnia.

Melatonin Receptor Agonists (e.g., Ramelteon)

Mimic the sleep hormone melatonin, helping regulate sleep cycles.

Over-the-counter (OTC) Sleep Aids (e.g., Diphenhydramine)

Common in allergy medications, causing drowsiness as a side effect.

Side Effects:

Drowsiness, grogginess, and memory issues.

Potential for dependency, especially with long-term use.

Risks: Short-term use is recommended; long-term use can cause tolerance and withdrawal.

Alcohol can intensify their effects.

Alternatives:

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) offers a non-drug treatment for sleep problems.

In short, sleeping pills should be used under medical supervision to avoid dependency and other risks.
